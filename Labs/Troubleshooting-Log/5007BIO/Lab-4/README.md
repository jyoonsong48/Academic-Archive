 ## Zone of Inhibition for Different Strain (MRSA and MSSA)
### Problem: Bacteria didn't grow -> no zone of inhibition
<br> Failed disc diffusion tests typically result from improper technique. Key factors include: uneven bacterial lawn inoculation, deviations from the standard 4mm agar depth, or fluctuations in incubation temperature and timing (Davis and Stout, 1971). Additionally, the use of expired antibiotic discs or incorrect storage conditions can lead to misleading zones of inhibition.

<br> However, In this specific experiment, complete absence of bacterial growth was observed. Evaporation during incubation can lead to water loss, resulting in decreased colony size and growth inhibition (Bonnet et al., 2019). But it is rarely the root cause of total failure across an entire cohort. Given that most samples failed to show growth, the issue is likely systemic rather than an isolated technical slip.

First possible cause can be suboptimal temperature settings. Ideal temperature for _Staphylococcus aureus_ is around 37°C, yet it can proliferate between 15°C-45°C (Missiakas and Schneewind, 2018), therefore temperature is unlikely the cause.
Another possible cause is use of the culture with incorrect inoculum density. The starting culture density could have been too low to establish a visible bacterial lawn. (The start density is unknown therefore it cannot be definitely stated that this is the primary cause.)

-> To improve the reliability of future data, the experiment should be repeated with strict adherence to McFarland standards for inoculum density and calibrated incubator monitoring to ensure consistent environmental parameters.
