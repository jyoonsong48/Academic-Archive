## Lab 1: Aseptic Techniques and Plating Methods
<br>1.	Skin Flora Assessment: Inoculate one blood agar plate with a "hand-print" before cleaning and a second plate after using a chosen hand hygiene product. Incubate inverted at 37°C.
<br>2.	Streak Plating: Use a sterilized bacteriological loop to create pure subcultures of E. coli and S. marcescens on separate nutrient agar plates using the streak plate technique to isolate single colonies.
<br>3.	Serial Dilutions: Perform 10-fold serial dilutions of an E. coli suspension ($2 \times 10^5$ CFU/ml) by transferring 0.1ml of culture into 0.9ml of sterile saline across a series of Eppendorf tubes.
<br>4.	Spread Plate Technique: Inoculate 0.1ml of specific dilutions onto nutrient agar plates and distribute evenly using a glass spreader sterilized with 70% alcohol and flame.

## Lab 2: Antimicrobial Susceptibility (Kirby-Bauer)
<br>1.	Lawn Preparation: Use sterile swabs to create a confluent lawn of A. baumannii and E. faecalis on standard antibiotic testing agar.
<br>2.	Disk Impregnation: Add 20µl of antibiotic stock solutions (Ciprofloxacin, Ampicillin, Meropenem, or Amikacin) to plain paper disks and allow them to dry for 15 minutes.
<br>3.	Testing: Apply the impregnated disks to the quadrants of the inoculated plates using sterile tweezers and incubate at 37°C overnight.

## Lab 3: Bacterial Identification & Differential Media
<br>1.	Gram Stain: Prepare a heat-fixed smear. Flood with crystal violet (30s), wash, apply iodine (30s), decolorize with 95% alcohol (20s), and counterstain with safranin (1 min).
<br>2.	Media Screening: Inoculate P. aeruginosa, S. aureus, E. coli, and S. faecalis onto MacConkey agar (selective for enteric/indicator for lactose fermentation), EMBA (selective for Gram-negative/indicator for E. coli), and Blood agar (enriched/indicator for haemolysis).

## Lab 4: Biochemical Tests, Natural Antimicrobials & MIC
<br>1.	Biochemical Identification: Perform the Oxidase test on Gram-negative species to detect cytochrome oxidase and the Catalase test on Gram-positive species using hydrogen peroxide to observe bubble formation.
<br>2.	Natural Products: Create an E. coli lawn, cut wells into the agar using a pipette tip, and fill them with crushed garlic or ginger to observe inhibition.
<br>3.	Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC): Prepare two-fold serial dilutions of Chloramphenicol and Gentamicin in Isosensitest broth within a multi-well plate. Inoculate with $10^{-2}$ dilutions of bacteria and incubate.
<br>4.	Crowded Plate Technique: Spread 100µl of a 1% soil suspension onto Nutrient and Streptomyces agar to screen for antibiotic-producing organisms.

## Lab 5: Phage Plaque Assay
<br>1.	Serial Dilution: Perform 10-fold serial dilutions of a T7 phage stock (pre-diluted 1:10,000) using E. coli C600 culture as the diluent. Incubate at 37°C for one hour.
<br>2.	Plaque Assay: Add 50µl of E. coli and 100µl of the phage dilutions into 3ml of molten soft agar (held at 45°C). Pour onto tryptone agar plates and allow to set.

## Lab 6: Morphological ID & Blood Staining
<br>1.	Hyphal Induction: Suspend C. albicans and C. glabrata in PBS with 10% Fetal Bovine Serum (FBS). Incubate at 37°C for 90–120 minutes and observe for hyphal growth.
<br>2.	Blood Film: Prick a finger with a lancet, place 5µl of blood on a slide, and spread into a thin film. Fix with methanol (10 min) and stain with Giemsa (10 min).
<br>3.	Protozoa ID: Prepare wet mounts of samples and identify "supergroups" based on motility organelles (cilia, flagella, pseudopodia).
